The Sparklesaurus: How pretty can help productivity

Think stickers are just for kids? Science says otherwise. Discover why aesthetic appeal isn't just decoration - it's a legitimate productivity tool that can transform your goal-tracking success.

These days I am a bit of a magpie. I am irresistibly drawn to all things sparkly and shiny. Oh, and sticky. Yes, dear readers, I have a confession to make: My name is Mrs Cranky and I am addicted to stickers.

I seem to have developed a sticker-seeking radar of late. It doesn't matter what shop I'm in or why I've gone there, if there is a rack of stickers in the vicinity, I will be drawn to it like iron filings to a magnet, and I will be leaving with at least several packs. Sometimes the sticker packs are the only thing I leave with, because whatever I originally went in there for has been erased from my mind by all the pretty shiny, puffy, colourful, glittery things.

I really am a hopeless case. The chances of me making a full recovery plummet by the day.

But, as these things go, this is not a bad problem to have. Even Mr Cranky – who rolls his eyes every time I show him my latest sticker acquisitions – agrees that it is better to have a spouse who compulsively buys $3 sticker packs than an addiction that could cost hundreds or thousands of dollars.

Anyway, the point is not that Mr Cranky is blessed to have me in his life and should thank his lucky stars on a daily basis, but that these silly little packets do for me what more sensible goal-tracking methods consistently failed to do: bring a bit of joy into the productivity process.

The science behind aesthetic productivity

This is more than just another example of 'Mrs Cranky Weirdness'. Research consistently shows that aesthetic appeal is not only about decoration – it's also a legitimate productivity tool. Studies have found that:

  • Employees who were able to decorate their own workspace were 32% more productive than employees who worked in bare spaces
  • Blue and green colour schemes in offices led to increased productivity and creativity among employees
  • Looking at visually appealing stimuli can improve people's performance in demanding tasks
  • Simply viewing beautiful images increases people's motivation to complete tasks

Neuroscience backs up these findings, too, showing that aesthetically pleasing elements activate brain regions responsible for linking behavioural outcomes to motivation and positive emotional responses.

I don't want to write too much about the research here, because the point of this post is for me to waffle on for way too long about my sticker collection, but there are links to studies at the end of this entry for anyone who is interested.

Anyway, back to the important stuff.

My sticker collection strategy

When I first started tracking, I used random stickers, but since I discovered how many different shapes there are, I've started buying specific sticker sets for specific sheets.

  • I use little metallic hearts for exercise goals to represent that I am doing something that is good for my heart. Actually, this is only partially true. I also bought a packet of yellow emojis with different expressions, which I will use when I implement my plan to get up early and exercise before work. (Spoiler: the angry-faced ones will definitely be used up before even a single smiley-face emoji sees the light of day!)
  • Latin is tracked using dinosaur stickers, as this ties in with my roller-skating dinosaur notebook. I could tell you that I also use dinosaur stickers to track the books about Ancient Rome I am reading to get more context for my Latin studies because it makes sense, but, truthfully, I am using them 'cos I have bought so many packets of prehistoric pests that my sticker container is swamped with them.
  • For tracking my progress learning Moonlight Sonata on the piano, I bought a roll of musical notes stickers.
  • Oh, and I just ordered a set of stickers with different cocktails on them. I have no idea what I will use these for, but I suspect whatever it is will need to be done sparingly, lest I be tempted to celebrate with a cocktail as well as a sticker.

Planning for future goals

Then there are all the stickers I need to buy for future tracking purposes.

  • I found a set of literature-inspired stickers that I will use for one of my planned goals for 2026: reading the original Grimm's Fairy Tales (the dark versions, not the sanitised versions that are told to little kids).
  • There are some gorgeous Van Gogh stickers that are perfect for my arts and crafts goals.
  • Then there are the ones I simply must have for holidays: ghosts and cute dogs in wizard hats for Halloween, and glittery Christmas trees, snowmen, and Santa stickers for Christmas. (Which, incidentally, I start celebrating in September, so consider this your early warning about the nature of many of my posts the second August is over.)

By the way, did I mention that I like stickers?

Beyond stickers: using color for motivation

Aesthetics doesn't need to start and end there, though. I print my tracking sheets on coloured paper, and I use different colours to track different things. For example:

  • Green is for exercise and health goals
  • Purple is for my most fun goals, 'cos it's my favourite colour
  • Pink is for any goal where my Inner Five-Year-Old takes over and starts waxing lyrical (i.e. bellowing) about the need for 'girly' things like fairies and unicorns

My Inner Five-Year-Old's decorating demands aside, stickers and coloured paper have brought an extra bit of visual satisfaction to my productivity progress. When I place one on a tracking sheet, I get a little burst of pleasure. They make me want to continue my goals so I can place more. As far as I'm concerned, anything that convinces me to floss regularly or open my Latin textbook is a worthwhile investment, even if it means I can no longer use my stationery drawer to hold actual stationery.
