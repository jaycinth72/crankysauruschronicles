The Demandasaurus: Why my Inner 5-Year-Old runs the show (and yours should, too)

Should you listen to your Inner Five-Year-Old about productivity decisions? After removing furniture to make room for Halloween stickers in September, I discovered that sometimes the most unreasonable demands lead to the most sustainable success.

It started innocently enough. These things always do.

Adult Brain: Right, I just need to print a simple tracking sheet for that new Great Courses series I want to start this weekend.

Inner Five-Year-Old: Ooh! A new tracking sheet! Can we use the Halloween stickers?

Adult Brain: It's the beginning of September. Halloween isn't for two months.

Inner Five-Year-Old: So? The pumpkin stickers are adorable! And the little ghost ones! And we got those sparkly orange ones that look like—

Adult Brain: We are not using Halloween stickers in September – that's completely unreasonable.

Inner Five-Year-Old: You know what's unreasonable? Letting perfectly good stickers sit in a drawer just because of some stupid calendar.

Adult Brain: But it's not actually Halloween yet.

The resolution: Why my Inner 5-Year-Old was right all along

And you know what happened next? I took down that coat hook. I printed those tracking sheets on brightly coloured paper. And I used those Halloween stickers in September without a shred of shame.

And it worked.

Not only did it work, it worked better than any sensible, seasonally-appropriate, minimalist tracking system I'd ever tried. The extra space meant I could see all my goals at once. The pretty-coloured paper caught my eye every time I went through the door. And those ridiculously out-of-season stickers? They made me smile every single time I added one.

More importantly, this little expansion project came at exactly the right time. After several stressful weeks dealing with my two unexpected family situations, my Inner Five-Year-Old knew exactly what I needed: more visual motivation, more pretty things, and more reasons to celebrate small wins.

My Adult Brain does still worry about concepts such as 'appropriate timing' and 'what people might think', but my I5YO was focused on the things that mattered: emotional recovery and sustainable motivation. Guess which approach actually helped me get back on track?

The deeper truth about Inner 5-Year-Old wisdom

Here's what I've learned from months of letting my Inner Five-Year-Old run the show: she's not actually irresponsible; she's just operating on a completely different – and often superior – understanding of human psychology.

Adult Brain says: Be sensible; follow the rules; do things the 'right' way.

Inner Five-Year-Old says: Make it fun; make it pretty; make it work for you.

Adult Brain says: Christmas stickers in September are inappropriate.

Inner Five-Year-Old says: Joy doesn't follow a calendar, and sparkly things make everything better.

Adult Brain says: You have enough tracking supplies.

Inner Five-Year-Old says: There's no such thing as too much motivation, and pretty things make hard tasks easier.

Adult Brain says: Taking down furniture for goal tracking is excessive.

Inner Five-Year-Old says: If it helps you achieve your dreams, how is it excessive?

And Rexie? Rexie just chomps through all the boring, sensible objections and backs up my Inner Five-Year-Old with his usual prehistoric authority.

Permission to be ruled by your inner child (sometimes)

The productivity world is full of advice about being disciplined, mature, and sensible. But sometimes the most productive thing you can do is let your Inner Five-Year-Old run the show.

Why? Because your Inner Five-Year-Old knows things that your Adult Brain has forgotten:

  • Enthusiasm is more sustainable than discipline
  • Pretty things make difficult tasks easier
  • Joy is a legitimate productivity tool
  • If something makes you happy and doesn't hurt anyone, seasonal appropriateness is irrelevant
  • Sometimes you need more supplies, not fewer
  • Visual motivation works better than internal motivation for many people
  • Celebrating small wins keeps you going better than waiting for big achievements

But isn't this just giving up when things get hard?

Now, I can already hear some of you thinking: "This sounds like you're just enabling yourself. What's stopping you from abandoning everything the moment it stops being fun?"

Fair question. And here's the honest answer: context and results.

When my Inner Five-Year-Old demanded Halloween stickers in September, Adult Brain thought it was frivolous nonsense. But what actually happened? I used those tracking sheets consistently for months. I achieved multiple goals. The system worked better than any 'sensible' approach I'd tried.

When she insisted on removing the coat hook for more door space, Adult Brain worried it was excessive. But the expanded visibility meant I could see all my goals at once, which meant I actually did more of them.

This isn't about abandoning things when they get difficult. It's about recognising when resistance comes from genuine misalignment rather than mere difficulty. There's a difference between:

Rexie-chomping-worthy resistance: "I don't feel like practicing piano today because I'm tired and it's hard."
Solution: CHOMP! Do it anyway. Small sticker for showing up.

Legitimate Inner Five-Year-Old wisdom: "I haven't opened my Latin textbook in three weeks because my brain genuinely wants to focus on seasonal projects right now, and forcing it is making me miserable."
Solution: Pivot temporarily. The Latin will still be there in January.

But what if your Inner Five-Year-Old wants to quit everything all the time?

Another valid concern. What if listening to your Inner Five-Year-Old just means never finishing anything?

Here's the thing: my Inner Five-Year-Old isn't actually irresponsible. She's selectively demanding. She fought for:

  • Wall-mounted tracking (which revolutionised my productivity)
  • Stickers (which keep me motivated months later)
  • Seasonal pivots (which prevent burnout and maintain long-term sustainability)

She did NOT demand that I:

  • Quit Ulysses when it got confusing (I adapted my approach instead)
  • Abandon Farsi when the lessons got harder (I kept going and finished the course)
  • Stop tracking health goals when I had migraines (I gave myself small stickers for showing up)

The difference? The first set of demands made the system more sustainable. The second set would have made me less successful.

Your Inner Five-Year-Old isn't trying to sabotage you. They're trying to help you build something you can actually maintain.

So how do you know when to listen?

Ask yourself these questions:

1. Has this request made my system more sustainable in the past? (Halloween stickers: yes; quitting everything: no.)

2. Am I avoiding difficulty, or am I recognising genuine misalignment? (Ulysses was difficult but I was engaged; Great Courses during festive season felt like a chore instead of a pleasure.)

3. What do my results say? (Wall-mounted tracking = 6+ months of consistent achievement; boring spreadsheets = one week, then abandonment.)

If your Inner Five-Year-Old's demand passes these tests, maybe they're onto something. And if you're still not sure? Try it for a month and see what happens. That's ROAR in action – Adaptable enough to experiment, Realistic enough to evaluate results.

And if your Inner Five-Year-Old does want to quit everything all of the time, revisit your goals and your motivation for doing them.

Ask yourself:

  • Are these actually MY goals, or goals I think I 'should' have?
  • Am I trying to force myself into someone else's version of productivity?
  • Have I built any joy into the process, or is it all grinding discipline?
  • Am I setting goals that work with my actual personality, or against it?

If your Inner Five-Year-Old is constantly in rebellion, they're probably trying to tell you something important: these goals might not be right for you, or the way you're pursuing them isn't sustainable.

That's not failure. That's valuable information.

The bottom line

So, the next time your Inner Five-Year-Old makes a completely unreasonable demand for more stickers, prettier tracking sheets, or Halloween decorations in September, maybe listen to them. They might just be the smartest productivity expert in your head.

After all, my Inner Five-Year-Old has been consistently right about what actually works; maybe yours is too.
